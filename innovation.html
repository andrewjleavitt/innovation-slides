When we fail, most rational people respond by:

trying to avoid dumb ideas

picking smart bets with clear impact the next time

the dead-end for innovation:

The self-same employees who are innovating and taking risks with you today are going to become risk averse and careful once they start failing — and sooner or later, they (and you) will fail.

What now?

Permission to fail

  • You don’t want to build your company with people who don’t care if they fail or not.
  • High standards
  • thirst for impact
  • “fire in the belly”
  • those same qualities that make us punish ourselves harshly for failing — those are precisely the traits that make amazing teammates who will put their heart into every project and give you the best chances of success.

Fail Fast

  • You can (and should) make every effort to fail as fast as possible
  • minimize the human costs of failure
  • tactic is limited by how long it realistically takes to prove or disprove the kind of ideas you work on.
  • Even ruthlessly optimizing project definition proof of concept, failing fast can still take months or years, especially if your innovation is technical

ancient, long-lost Uncle Tru-ism:

“well, we don’t penalize failure.”

  • You don’t have to.
  • Even if you don’t fire employees or dock their bonuses for working on a failed project, the kind of people who care deeply and passionately about their work will penalize themselves, with feelings of shame and loss.

Nurture the spirit of innovation

hire people who hate to fail, and build a culture that picks people up, dusts them off, and encourages them to try again when failure occurs.
